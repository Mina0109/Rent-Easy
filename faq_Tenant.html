Frequently Asked Questions - UK Rental Laws

For Landlords

As a landlord in the UK, you are legally required to ensure that the property is safe and habitable. This includes maintaining the structure and exterior, ensuring that all installations for the supply of water, gas, electricity, and sanitation are working, and that the property is free from health hazards.

The process for increasing rent depends on the type of tenancy agreement in place. For periodic tenancies (rolling week by week or month by month), landlords can typically increase the rent once a year, with proper notice. For fixed-term tenancies, rent can only be increased if the tenant agrees or if there is a rent review clause in the agreement.

To evict a tenant for not paying rent, you must follow the proper legal procedures, which include serving a Section 8 notice, followed by court action if the tenant does not leave by the specified date.

Landlords must place a tenant's deposit in a government-approved tenancy deposit protection (TDP) scheme within 30 days of receiving it and provide the tenant with the prescribed information about where the deposit is held.

Landlords have the right to enter the property for inspections or to carry out repairs, but they must give the tenant 24 hours' notice and conduct the visit at a reasonable time of day, unless it's an emergency.

For Tenants

Report any issues to your landlord as soon as possible. The landlord is typically responsible for repairs to the property's structure and utilities. Ensure that you communicate in writing to have a record of your correspondence.

The notice period you need to give will depend on the type of tenancy agreement you have. It's usually at least one month for a monthly rolling contract, but this should be detailed in your tenancy agreement.

If your landlord has not protected your deposit in a TDP scheme, you can take them to court. The court can order the landlord to pay up to three times the deposit amount as compensation.

For a fixed-term tenancy, your landlord can only increase the rent if you agree or if there is a clause in your agreement allowing it. For a periodic tenancy, the landlord can propose a rent increase, but you can challenge it if it's not reasonable.

If you believe charges taken from your deposit are unjust, you can raise a dispute with the tenancy deposit protection scheme holding your deposit. They will provide a free dispute resolution service.
